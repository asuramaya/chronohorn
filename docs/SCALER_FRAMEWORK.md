# Scaler Framework

`Chronohorn` is not just a new compressor.
It is a scaler framework for rescuing weird ideas from `Conker` and `BLINX` without rescuing the invalid mechanisms that originally carried them.

## Parent Signals

### Conker

`Conker` produced two kinds of lessons:

1. invalid but informative absurd-score lessons
2. valid runtime and artifact-boundary lessons

The absurd-score branches were not random noise.
They pointed at real hidden structure:

- tiny control surfaces mattered far more than expected
- future-aware exactness was extraordinarily valuable
- apparently smooth mask geometry could carry razor-sharp functional structure
- dense causal "holograms" could look almost trivial while encoding most of the behavior

What survives from `Conker`:

- packed-memory first
- score-before-update discipline
- artifact-boundary seriousness
- narrow, high-precision control signals

What does not survive:

- learned eval-time structural masks
- trainable causal holograms
- contaminated teacher/runtime boundaries

### BLINX

`BLINX` produced a different kind of absurdity:

- not absurd legal `bpb`
- absurd removable structure

The important result was not that `BLINX` compressed well.
It did not.
The important result was:

- bidirectional structure is abundant
- exact uniqueness is too strict
- small candidate sets are the real signal
- future-only uplift and self-inclusion contamination are large enough that oracle labels must be cleaned before use

What survives from `BLINX`:

- leave-one-out structural analysis
- contamination adjustment
- candidate-set labels
- noncausal teacher signals

What does not survive:

- BLINX as a runtime scorer
- BLINX as a direct codec claim

## Rescued Weird Ideas

The framework should explicitly preserve these weird ideas:

1. **Hologram intuition**
- a nearly trivial-looking surface can hide most of the behavior
- therefore tiny deviations, overlaps, and residuals matter
- treat control surfaces as first-class objects

2. **Future-aware exactness**
- future information is genuinely valuable
- but it must be used only for offline supervision
- the bridge must distill it into legal causal behavior

3. **Computation through removal**
- structural simplification is a real signal even when it is not a good codec
- BLINX-style removal survives as a target generator, not a runtime

4. **Orthogonal experts**
- the best gains do not come from squeezing the same posterior harder
- they come from genuinely different causal channels such as `match` and `skip`

5. **Attack the miracle**
- absurd scores and absurd removal fractions should be treated as invitations to attack, not to celebrate
- if something survives the attack, that residue is the real idea

## Chronohorn As Scaler

`Chronohorn` scales the rescued ideas by keeping four roles hard:

1. `oracle`
- offline only
- BLINX inheritance

2. `compressor`
- causal runtime
- Conker inheritance

3. `bridge`
- Giddy-Up inheritance
- where noncausal structure becomes legal runtime leverage

4. `audit`
- adversarial pressure on every boundary

So the scaler question is not:

- what new expert should we add?

It is:

- how much of the weird parent signal survives after we force it through a legal bridge into a causal compressor?

## Current Working Interpretation

The current live answer is:

- `match+skip` is the best surviving runtime family so far
- BLINX-style small-candidate-set structure is still the best oracle surface
- richer bridge features do help a little
- the remaining gap is now bridge quality and scale, not a search for another contaminated miracle

## Practical Build Rule

For any new weird idea:

1. state which role it belongs to
2. forbid it from leaking across roles
3. attack it until the absurd part dies
4. keep the residue if it still buys audited `bpb`

That is how `Chronohorn` turns `Conker` and `BLINX` from a history of weird branches into a real scaler framework.
